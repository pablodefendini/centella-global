# Alianza de País — speaker notes

**Talk:** Alianza de País — How a Puerto Rican coalition reframed sovereignty as a question of governance
**Venue:** Centella Global Forum, 2026
**Length:** 7–8 min target (5–10 min budget)
**Through-line:** Narrative + identity. The Alianza did not solve Puerto Rico's status problem. It refused to argue it. It moved the conversation to a question voters were already living, and pulled people across old lines into a coalition.

## Slide 1 — Cover · ~30 sec

**Beat:** Open warm. Land the title and the question.

- Greet the room. Thank Centella for hosting.
- Say what you're going to do: tell the story of one coalition, in one place, for one election — because the *shape* of the problem they faced is the shape a lot of you are working on right now.
- "The place is Puerto Rico. The coalition is called Alianza de País. The election was 2024. And the lesson, if there is one, isn't that they won. They didn't. The lesson is what they refused to argue about."

---

## Slide 2 — The trap · ~60 sec

**Beat:** Set up the wedge. Make it visceral that the trap is *generations* old.

- For seventy years, Puerto Rican electoral politics has run on three letters: status. Statehood, commonwealth, independence. Three options, two parties holding two of them, and any party that took the third — sovereignty — locked at three to five percent of the vote, every cycle, no matter who was on the ballot or what the issue of the day was.
- The trap isn't that voters disagreed with the independentista position on governance. Many agreed. The trap is that the status question swallowed every other question.
- "If you're working on a movement anywhere, you know this shape. There is some single argument — about identity, about belonging, about the past — that absorbs every conversation about the future. That's the trap."

> **[FILL: optional — one concrete example of the trap in your own family / your own circle. The kind of voter who agreed on everything except status, and so wouldn't vote for PIP. Two sentences. This grounds it.]**

## Slide 3 — Two parties, two DNAs · ~50 sec

**Beat:** The unlikeliness of the merger. Make the audience feel the friction.

- Two parties came together to form the Alianza. They look similar from a distance. Up close they were not.
- **PIP** — decades-old, independentista by name and by record, a base built around a constitutional position. Loyal, organized, ceiling-locked.
- **MVC** — younger, formed around anti-corruption and good governance, deliberately cross-ideological. People in MVC agreed on nothing about status and a lot about how the island was being run.
- Coming together was not obvious. Different cultures, different bases, different theories of how change happens.

> **[FILL: the friction inside the room. Was there a specific decision or a specific person whose call made the merger possible? Was there a moment it almost broke? Two sentences. The forum audience knows what coalition friction feels like — give them a glimpse.]**

---

## Slide 4 — The reframe · ~60 sec  *(this is the pivot of the talk)*

**Beat:** Land the central move. Slow down. This is the slide the audience should remember.

- Here's the move that mattered. Not "what should Puerto Rico be." That question had been asked and lost for seventy years. The Alianza substituted a different question: **what can we govern?**
- That sounds small. It is not small. It moves the entire conversation off a question voters had answered in their bones a long time ago — and were tired of being asked — and onto a question they were living with every day.
- Once the question changes, the coalition shape changes. Status liberals and status conservatives can disagree about the constitution and still agree that the lights should stay on.

> **[FILL: who first articulated the reframe inside the room — was it a specific operative, a candidate, a conversation? If it has a story, tell it in two sentences. If it doesn't have a clean origin, say that — "this came together over months, no one person owns it." Either is honest.]**

---

## Slide 5 — País · ~45 sec

**Beat:** The word does work. Show how.

- The word "país" — country — was load-bearing. It said: *we are already a country*. The question is not whether we will become one. The question is whether we can govern ourselves with dignity.
- That single word lets the coalition claim national identity without forcing voters to commit to a status outcome upfront. Statehood voters could say "país" and mean a thing. Independence voters could say "país" and mean something else. Both could mean it together.
- "Identity is a container. The Alianza found a container that fit more people than the old one."

---

## Slide 6 — The case the duopoly already made · ~50 sec

**Beat:** Specifics. The reason the reframe worked is that the proof was already on the floor.

- The Alianza didn't have to make the case for governance failure. The duopoly had spent a decade making it for them.
- Walk the list — fast, conversational. Don't read it.
  - Energy grid that fails routinely
  - School system in retreat
  - Brain drain that gets worse every year
  - A fiscal control board imposed from outside
  - A public sector hollowed by austerity
  - Corruption case after corruption case — both parties, all levels
- "Every one of those is a governance problem. Not a status problem. The Alianza could speak to all of it without ever saying the word 'independence' on the campaign trail."

---

## Slide 7 — Result · ~30 sec

**Beat:** Don't oversell. State it clean.

- Juan Dalmau, the Alianza's gubernatorial candidate, finished second.
- That sentence is heavier than it sounds. It is the first time in the modern political history of the island that a candidate from outside the PNP/PPD duopoly came that close to the governor's mansion.
- They didn't win. The point isn't that they won. The point is that the framework worked — at the ballot box, at scale, in a real race.

> **[FILL: confirm the exact numbers if you want them on stage — Dalmau's vote share, the margin, turnout differential. Or leave it as "second" and let the shape do the work. You'll know which is right for the room.]**

---

## Slide 8 — The lesson · ~50 sec

**Beat:** Generalize, but earn it. This is for the room.

- Here's the part that travels. When a wedge issue locks your movement out of governance, the temptation is to argue the wedge harder. To prove your side of it. To campaign on it.
- The Alianza did the opposite. It refused the wedge. It moved the conversation to a question voters were already living, and let the answer pull people into a coalition they would not have joined under the old frame.
- "Identity work *before* policy work. Not after. The frame has to fit before the platform can land."

---

## Slide 9 — Unfinished · ~40 sec

**Beat:** Honesty. Don't sell a clean ending. Open the door for the audience.

- I want to be careful with the lesson. The framework worked at the ballot box. Whether it survives the messy work of post-election coalition governance — whether the two parties can actually govern together when they disagree, whether 2028 brings a real majority instead of a strong second — is unfinished.
- The story isn't that the Alianza solved the trap. The story is that it found a door.
- "Many of you are working on your own version of this trap. The shape repeats. The doors don't look the same. But they're there."
